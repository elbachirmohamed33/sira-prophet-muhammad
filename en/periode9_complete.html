The First Pledge of Aqaba

621 CE

The First Ansar Accept Islam

The Beginning of Medina's Embrace of Islam

üèõÔ∏è The Context of Pilgrimage Season

After the miraculous Night Journey (Isra and Mi'raj), the Prophet Ô∑∫ continued his efforts to find supporters outside Mecca. Each year during the pilgrimage season, he would approach various Arab tribes visiting Mecca, inviting them to Islam and seeking their protection and support. These efforts had largely been unsuccessful, with most tribes rejecting his message due to fear of the powerful Quraysh.

During the pilgrimage season of 621 CE (the 11th year of prophethood), the Prophet Ô∑∫ encountered a small group of men from the Khazraj tribe of Yathrib (later called Medina). This meeting would prove to be one of the most significant encounters in Islamic history, marking the beginning of Islam's expansion beyond Mecca and the eventual establishment of the first Islamic state.

üèòÔ∏è The Situation in Yathrib

Yathrib was a city located about 400 kilometers north of Mecca, inhabited by several Arab tribes (mainly Aws and Khazraj) and three Jewish tribes (Banu Qaynuqa, Banu Nadir, and Banu Qurayzah). The city had been plagued by decades of tribal warfare, particularly between the Aws and Khazraj, which had weakened both sides and created a longing for peace and unity.

The Jewish tribes in Yathrib possessed knowledge of previous scriptures and often spoke about the coming of a final prophet. They would sometimes threaten the Arab tribes by saying that when this prophet arrived, they would follow him and defeat their enemies. This created an expectation among the Arabs of Yathrib about the appearance of a promised prophet, making them more receptive to the Prophet Muhammad's Ô∑∫ message.

"And it is He who has sent among the unlettered a Messenger from themselves reciting to them His verses and purifying them and teaching them the Book and wisdom."
Quran 62:2

üë• The First Meeting

The encounter took place at Aqaba, a valley outside Mecca where pilgrims would camp. The Prophet Ô∑∫ approached six men from the Khazraj tribe who were performing their pilgrimage rituals. These men were: As'ad ibn Zurara, Awf ibn al-Harith, Rafi ibn Malik, Qutba ibn Amir, Uqba ibn Amir, and Jabir ibn Abdullah (some sources vary on the exact names).

When the Prophet Ô∑∫ presented Islam to them, explaining the oneness of Allah, the Day of Judgment, and his role as Allah's final messenger, the men were immediately struck by the truth of his message. They recognized that this could be the prophet their Jewish neighbors had been expecting, and they saw in Islam a potential solution to their tribal conflicts and spiritual needs.

‚ú® Recognition of the Truth

The Khazraj delegation was particularly receptive to the Islamic message because of their familiarity with monotheistic concepts through their Jewish neighbors. When they heard the Quran being recited, they were moved by its beauty and spiritual power, recognizing it as divine revelation. They also appreciated the Prophet's Ô∑∫ emphasis on justice, equality, and moral conduct, which aligned with their desire for social reform.

As'ad ibn Zurara, who emerged as the leader of the group, was especially impressed by the Prophet's Ô∑∫ character and message. He and his companions recognized that Islam offered exactly what their troubled city needed: a unifying faith that could transcend tribal divisions and establish justice and peace among all inhabitants of Yathrib.

ü§ù The Pledge of Allegiance

After listening to the Prophet Ô∑∫ and being convinced of the truth of Islam, the six men embraced the faith and pledged their allegiance to him. This first pledge was primarily a declaration of faith and personal commitment to Islamic principles. They promised to worship Allah alone, abandon idolatry, and follow the moral and ethical teachings of Islam.

The terms of this initial pledge were relatively simple and focused on spiritual and moral transformation rather than political or military commitments. The new Muslims promised to avoid shirk (associating partners with Allah), theft, adultery, infanticide, lying, and disobedience to the Prophet Ô∑∫ in matters of righteousness. This pledge established the foundation for a larger commitment that would come the following year.

"As'ad ibn Zurara said to his companions: 'Do you know what you are pledging yourselves to? You are pledging to wage war against all mankind. If you think you can fulfill this, then proceed. But if you fear failure, then abandon it now, for it would be more honorable before Allah.'"
Ibn Hisham in As-Sirah

üìö Mus'ab ibn Umayr's Mission

Recognizing the potential of this opening, the Prophet Ô∑∫ decided to send a qualified teacher to Yathrib to educate the new Muslims and spread Islam among the population. He chose Mus'ab ibn Umayr, a young, intelligent, and well-educated companion who had shown exceptional dedication to Islam despite coming from a wealthy family and sacrificing his comfortable lifestyle for the faith.

Mus'ab ibn Umayr, known as "Al-Muqri" (the Reciter), was an ideal choice for this mission. He possessed excellent knowledge of the Quran, deep understanding of Islamic teachings, noble character, and the social skills necessary to interact with different segments of Yathrib's society. His mission would prove to be one of the most successful early Islamic missionary efforts.

üè† Hospitality of As'ad ibn Zurara

When Mus'ab arrived in Yathrib, he was hosted by As'ad ibn Zurara, who provided him with lodging and introduced him throughout the city. As'ad's enthusiasm for Islam and his prominent position in Khazraj society helped open doors for Mus'ab that might otherwise have remained closed. Together, they began a systematic campaign to spread Islam among both the Aws and Khazraj tribes.

As'ad and Mus'ab would visit homes, sit in gatherings, attend tribal meetings, and engage in discussions about Islam with anyone willing to listen. Their approach was gentle, persuasive, and respectful of local customs and sensitivities. They emphasized Islam's message of unity, justice, and spiritual fulfillment, showing how the faith could address the specific problems facing Yathrib's inhabitants.

üéØ Strategic Conversions

Mus'ab's mission achieved remarkable success through a combination of strategic targeting and divine guidance. He focused on influential leaders and young men who could become advocates for Islam within their respective tribes and families. His most significant early conversion was Sa'd ibn Mu'adh, the chief of Banu Abd al-Ashhal (a clan of the Aws tribe), whose acceptance of Islam led to the conversion of his entire clan.

Another crucial conversion was that of Usayd ibn Hudayr, a respected leader and skilled orator who initially came to confront Mus'ab but ended up embracing Islam after hearing the Quran. These high-profile conversions created a domino effect, as other tribal members followed their leaders into Islam, and the new faith began to spread rapidly throughout Yathrib.

"And those who believed and emigrated and fought in the cause of Allah and those who gave shelter and aided - it is they who are the believers, truly."
Quran 8:74

üïå Establishing Islamic Practices

As the number of Muslims in Yathrib grew, Mus'ab began establishing regular Islamic practices and community structures. He organized congregational prayers, taught Quranic recitation, explained Islamic law and ethics, and helped resolve disputes according to Islamic principles. These activities demonstrated Islam's practical benefits for community life and social organization.

The new Muslim community in Yathrib began to experience the transformative effects of Islam. Former enemies became brothers in faith, tribal conflicts began to subside, and a sense of moral purpose and spiritual fulfillment replaced the previous atmosphere of endless warfare and revenge. This transformation would be crucial for Islam's future success in the city.

üìà Rapid Growth of Islam

Within one year of the first pledge at Aqaba, Islam had spread extensively throughout Yathrib. Entire clans converted, including many from both the Aws and Khazraj tribes. The success was so dramatic that Mus'ab reported to the Prophet Ô∑∫ that there were very few houses in Yathrib where Islam had not entered, and the Muslim community was becoming a significant force in the city's politics and social life.

The rapid spread of Islam in Yathrib contrasted sharply with the slow progress in Mecca, where the Quraysh's opposition remained fierce and conversions were difficult and dangerous. This success gave the Muslim community hope and confidence that Islam could find acceptance in other Arab communities, potentially providing an alternative to the hostility they faced in their homeland.

ü§î Opposition and Challenges

Despite its success, the Islamic mission in Yathrib faced several challenges. Some tribal leaders resisted the new faith, fearing it would undermine their traditional authority and customs. The Jewish tribes watched the spread of Islam with concern, realizing that this might be the prophet they had been expecting but whose message differed from their expectations and interests.

Additionally, some Arabs in Yathrib worried about the political implications of accepting a prophet from Mecca, fearing it might compromise their city's independence or drag them into conflict with the powerful Quraysh. These concerns would need to be addressed in future negotiations between the Prophet Ô∑∫ and the Yathrib delegation.

"Mus'ab ibn Umayr recited Surah Yusuf to Sa'd ibn Mu'adh and Usayd ibn Hudayr. When they heard it, their faces lit up with the light of Islam, and they said: 'How beautiful and excellent is this speech!'"
At-Tabari in his Tarikh

üóìÔ∏è Preparing for the Second Pledge

As the pilgrimage season of 622 CE approached, the Muslims of Yathrib began preparing to send a larger and more representative delegation to meet with the Prophet Ô∑∫. They had spent a year learning about Islam, establishing their community, and considering the implications of a deeper commitment to the Prophet Ô∑∫ and his mission.

The success of the first year had convinced many in Yathrib that Islam offered the solution to their city's problems and that the Prophet Ô∑∫ might be the leader who could unite their divided community. However, they also understood that inviting him to their city would involve greater responsibilities and potential risks, requiring careful consideration and broad consensus among the tribes.
