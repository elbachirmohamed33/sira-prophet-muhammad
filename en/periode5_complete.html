First Emigration to Abyssinia

615 CE

Seeking Refuge in a Just Kingdom

The First Test of Faith Through Migration

üî• Escalating Persecution in Mecca

By the year 615 CE, five years after the first revelation, the persecution of Muslims in Mecca had intensified dramatically. What had begun as mockery and social ostracism had evolved into systematic torture, economic boycotts, and physical violence. The Quraysh leadership, realizing that Islam was gaining followers despite their opposition, decided to crush the movement through increasingly brutal methods.

The most vulnerable Muslims - slaves, freedmen, and those without strong tribal protection - suffered the worst abuse. People like Bilal ibn Rabah Ô∑∫ were tortured under the scorching sun, Yasir and Sumayyah (the first martyrs in Islam) were killed for their faith, and many others faced daily humiliation and violence. The situation had become unbearable for many of the believers.

ü§î The Prophet's Difficult Decision

The Prophet Muhammad Ô∑∫ watched with deep pain as his followers suffered for their faith. As their leader and the recipient of Allah's revelation, he felt responsible for their welfare. After consulting with his closest companions and seeking guidance through prayer, he made a momentous decision: some of the Muslims should migrate to a place where they could practice their religion freely and live in safety.

This decision was not made lightly. Migration meant leaving behind family, property, and familiar surroundings. It meant venturing into the unknown, relying on the mercy of foreign rulers, and facing an uncertain future. However, the Prophet Ô∑∫ understood that the preservation of faith and the protection of believers were paramount concerns that justified this difficult step.

üåç Why Abyssinia?

The Prophet Ô∑∫ chose Abyssinia (modern-day Ethiopia) as the destination for this first migration for several strategic reasons. Abyssinia was ruled by the Negus (King) Ashama ibn Abjar, who was known throughout the Arabian Peninsula for his justice, fairness, and protection of the oppressed. The kingdom had a reputation as a place where religious minorities could find sanctuary and live peacefully.

Additionally, Abyssinia was a Christian kingdom, and Christians were considered "People of the Book" in Islamic understanding - monotheists who worshipped the same God as Muslims, even if their specific beliefs differed. The Prophet Ô∑∫ believed that the Christians of Abyssinia would be more sympathetic to Muslim beliefs than the idol-worshipping Quraysh of Mecca.

"And whoever emigrates for the cause of Allah will find on the earth many locations and abundance. And whoever leaves his home as an emigrant to Allah and His Messenger and then death overtakes him - his reward has already become incumbent upon Allah."
Quran 4:100

üë• The First Group of Emigrants

The first group of emigrants consisted of eleven men and four women, carefully selected by the Prophet Ô∑∫ based on their circumstances and needs. The group was led by Uthman ibn Affan Ô∑∫ (who would later become the third Caliph) and included his wife Ruqayyah Ô∑∫, the Prophet's daughter. This demonstrated the Prophet's trust in Uthman and the importance he placed on this mission.

Other notable emigrants included Abdur Rahman ibn Awf Ô∑∫, Zubayr ibn al-Awwam Ô∑∫, Musab ibn Umayr Ô∑∫, and Abu Hudhayfah ibn Utbah Ô∑∫. These were mostly young men and women from various tribes, representing a cross-section of the early Muslim community. The diversity of the group showed that Islam transcended tribal boundaries and united people from different backgrounds.

‚õµ The Secret Journey

The emigration had to be conducted in complete secrecy to avoid interference from the Quraysh. The emigrants left Mecca in small groups at different times, using various routes to avoid detection. They made their way to the Red Sea coast, where they found passage on trading ships bound for the ports of Abyssinia. The journey was fraught with danger and uncertainty.

The sea voyage itself was perilous, as these early Muslims had little experience with maritime travel. They faced storms, the risk of piracy, and the constant fear that Quraysh agents might pursue them. However, their faith sustained them through these trials, and they saw their journey as a test from Allah that would ultimately lead to relief and safety.

üëë Reception by the Negus

When the Muslim emigrants arrived in Abyssinia, they sought an audience with the Negus, King Ashama ibn Abjar. The king received them with curiosity and kindness, asking them about their religion and the reasons for their migration. The Muslims, led by Ja'far ibn Abi Talib Ô∑∫ (who had joined them later), presented their case with eloquence and honesty.

They explained that they had been living in ignorance, worshipping idols, eating carrion, committing shameful acts, and oppressing the weak. Then Allah had sent them a messenger from among themselves, teaching them to worship Allah alone, to be truthful and trustworthy, to maintain family ties, to be good neighbors, and to avoid all that was forbidden. They were being persecuted for these beliefs and sought refuge in his just kingdom.

"Umm Salamah Ô∑∫ reported: When we reached Abyssinia, the Negus gave us the best of neighborhoods. We practiced our religion safely, worshipped Allah without harm, and spoke nothing but good."
Ibn Hisham's Sirah

üïå Life in Abyssinia

The Negus granted the Muslims asylum and allowed them to settle in his kingdom. They established a small but thriving community, where they could practice their religion openly, perform their prayers without fear, and live according to Islamic principles. This was the first time since the early days of Islam that Muslims could worship Allah freely without the threat of persecution.

The emigrants integrated well into Abyssinian society while maintaining their Islamic identity. They learned the local languages, engaged in trade and other professions, and earned the respect of their neighbors through their honest dealings and good character. Their presence served as an early example of Muslim minorities living peacefully in non-Muslim societies.

üì∞ The False Report

After some time in Abyssinia, the emigrants received news that caused great excitement: they heard that the people of Mecca had accepted Islam and that the persecution had ended. This news, though later proven false, led most of the emigrants to make the decision to return to Mecca to be reunited with their families and to participate in what they believed was the triumph of Islam in their homeland.

The journey back to Mecca was undertaken with joy and hope. However, as they approached their hometown, they discovered that the reports had been greatly exaggerated. While there had been some positive developments, the fundamental opposition of the Quraysh leadership to Islam remained unchanged, and the persecution continued, though perhaps with less intensity than before.

‚öñÔ∏è The Quraysh Embassy to Abyssinia

Meanwhile, the Quraysh leadership in Mecca was furious about the Muslims finding sanctuary in Abyssinia. They decided to send an embassy to the Negus, led by Amr ibn al-As (who would later convert to Islam) and Abdullah ibn Abi Rabia, with valuable gifts and a request to return the Muslim refugees to Mecca for punishment.

The Quraysh representatives presented their case to the Negus, claiming that the Muslims were troublemakers who had abandoned the religion of their ancestors and were spreading discord. They argued that these refugees should be returned to face justice in their homeland. The embassy brought costly gifts to influence the king's decision and lobbied with his courtiers and bishops.

üó£Ô∏è Ja'far's Eloquent Defense

The Negus, being a just ruler, decided to hear both sides before making a decision. He summoned the Muslim emigrants, now led by Ja'far ibn Abi Talib Ô∑∫ (the Prophet's cousin and Ali's brother), to present their case. Ja'far gave one of the most eloquent and moving speeches in early Islamic history, explaining the nature of Islam and their situation.

Ja'far said: "O King, we were a people in a state of ignorance and immorality, worshipping idols and eating carrion, committing all sorts of shameful acts, breaking the ties of kinship, treating guests badly, and the strong among us exploited the weak. We remained in this state until Allah sent us a messenger from among ourselves, whose lineage, truthfulness, trustworthiness, and integrity we knew. He called us to worship Allah alone and to cast aside the stones and idols that we and our fathers used to worship besides Allah."

"And those who emigrated for the cause of Allah after they had been wronged - We will surely settle them in this world in a good place; but the reward of the Hereafter is greater, if only they could know."
Quran 16:41

üìñ The Recitation of Surah Maryam

When the Negus asked Ja'far to recite something from their scripture, Ja'far chose to recite verses from Surah Maryam (Chapter of Mary), which tells the story of Jesus Ô∑∫ and his mother Mary Ô∑∫. The choice was strategically brilliant, as it presented Islamic beliefs about Jesus in a way that resonated with Christian understanding while maintaining Islamic monotheistic principles.

As Ja'far recited the beautiful verses describing the birth of Jesus, his miracles, and his message, the Negus and his bishops were moved to tears. The king declared: "This and what Jesus brought come from the same source." He then announced that he would not surrender the Muslims to the Quraysh embassy, and he returned their gifts, saying he wanted nothing from them.

‚úä The Prostration Controversy

The Quraysh representatives, frustrated by their initial failure, tried another approach the next day. They told the Negus that the Muslims held blasphemous beliefs about Jesus Ô∑∫, claiming that Muslims said Jesus was merely a servant of Allah rather than the son of God. This was a more serious theological challenge that could potentially turn the Christian king against the Muslims.

When confronted with this question, Ja'far responded with the Islamic position: "We say about Jesus what our Prophet has told us: he is the servant of Allah, His messenger, His spirit, and His word, which He cast into Mary, the virgin, the good, the pure." The Negus, hearing this, picked up a stick and said: "Jesus, son of Mary, does not exceed what you have said by the length of this stick." His bishops protested, but the king remained firm in his decision to protect the Muslims.

üè° Establishment of the Muslim Community

With the Negus's protection secured, the Muslim community in Abyssinia began to establish itself more permanently. They built relationships with local communities, engaged in trade and various professions, and created a sustainable way of life in their new homeland. Some married local women who converted to Islam, further integrating into Abyssinian society.

The community served as an important precedent for later Muslim expansion and integration into diverse societies. They demonstrated that Muslims could maintain their religious identity while respecting local customs and contributing positively to their host communities. Their success in Abyssinia provided a model for Islamic minorities in non-Muslim lands.
